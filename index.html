Video-Based Optimal Transport for Feedback-Efficient Offline Preference-Based Reinforcement Learning

Tung M. Luu, Hwanhee Kim, Younghwan Lee, Chang D. Yoo
Korea Advanced Institute of Science and Technology (KAIST)

ICML 2026 (Oral)

arXiv OpenReview Code

Abstract

Conveying complex objectives to reinforcement learning (RL) agents often requires meticulous reward engineering. Preference-based RL (PbRL) offers a promising alternative by learning reward functions from human feedback, but its scalability is hindered by high labeling costs. Inspired by advances in Video Foundation Models (ViFMs), we present Video-based Optimal Transport Preference (VOTP), a semi-supervised framework that learns effective reward functions from only a handful of labels. By leveraging optimal transport to align visual trajectories within the rich representation space of ViFMs, VOTP effectively generates high-fidelity pseudo-labels for large amounts of unlabeled data, substantially reducing human supervision. Extensive experiments across locomotion and manipulation benchmarks demonstrate the superiority of VOTP, which outperforms state-of-the-art offline PbRL methods under limited feedback budgets. We also showcase the robustness of VOTP in the presence of visual distractors and validate its utility on real robotic tasks, where it learns meaningful rewards with minimal human input.

VOTP Method

VOTP is a semi-supervised framework for offline preference-based RL that learns an effective reward function from only a handful of human preference labels. The key idea is to use optimal transport (OT) to align visual trajectories within the rich representation space of Video Foundation Models (ViFMs), turning a small labeled set into high-fidelity supervision over a large pool of unlabeled trajectories.



Given a small set of human-labeled trajectory pairs and a large set of unlabeled trajectories, VOTP embeds each trajectory in the representation space of a pretrained ViFM. It then computes an optimal-transport alignment between unlabeled trajectories and labeled references, and uses the resulting alignment cost to generate high-fidelity pseudo-labels for the unlabeled data. Training the reward model on both the human labels and these pseudo-labels substantially reduces the required human supervision. The learned reward is then used to optimize the policy with an offline RL algorithm.

Results

We evaluate VOTP across locomotion and manipulation benchmarks under limited feedback budgets, comparing against state-of-the-art offline preference-based RL methods. We further study robustness to visual distractors and validate VOTP on real robotic tasks.

Comparison to Baselines

We compare VOTP with state-of-the-art offline preference-based RL baselines across locomotion and manipulation tasks. The results show that VOTP achieves stronger policy performance under limited human feedback by expanding sparse preference labels into reliable pseudo-supervision.

Effect of OT to infer pseudo-labels

This analysis isolates the contribution of optimal transport in pseudo-label inference. By aligning trajectories in the video representation space, OT provides more informative labels for unlabeled trajectory pairs and improves reward learning quality.

Comparison with various trajectory encoders

We evaluate how different trajectory encoders affect preference propagation and downstream policy learning. Strong video representations help VOTP capture task progress more robustly than encoders that are less aligned with temporal visual behavior.

Comparison along the Preference Feedback Number

We study performance as the number of human preference labels changes. VOTP is designed for feedback efficiency, and the comparison highlights its ability to maintain strong performance even when only a small number of labeled trajectory pairs is available.

Pearson correlation between learned rewards and GT rewards

We measure the Pearson correlation between learned rewards and ground-truth rewards to assess whether the reward model captures true task progress. Higher correlation indicates that VOTP learns reward signals that better reflect the underlying objective.

Visualization of learned reward

We visualize the learned reward along representative trajectories. The reward profiles show whether the model assigns increasing or meaningful reward as the agent completes task-relevant behaviors.
